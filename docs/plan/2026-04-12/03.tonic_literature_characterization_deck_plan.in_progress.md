## Design goals

1. Optimize for **self-study clarity**, not for a 9–12 minute spoken update.
2. Allow major papers to span multiple slides when that makes the mechanism or method clearer.
3. For each major paper, make the reader able to answer six questions: problem, method/mechanism, evaluation setup, concrete findings, interpretation, and limitations.
3a. For each major paper, the mechanism slide should identify the **most interesting technique / design choice** in a way that stands on its own as a learning artifact, not only as a repo takeaway.
4. Prefer simple pipelines, staged diagrams, compact comparison tables, and structured cards over report-like prose.
5. Keep every claim grounded in the already collected repo sources and the paper-detail notes already reflected in the deck.
6. Preserve the repo's April Typst vocabulary where convenient (`callout`, `panel`, `card`, `stage-card`), but allow more slides and more explanation.
7. Reframe away from meeting-talk constraints: the deck should feel like a guided walkthrough, not a compressed status update.
8. Each major paper module must include at least one mechanism or methodology visual and one result-oriented slide component.
9. RR-Compound must stay explicitly low-confidence and qualitative unless a stronger paper-ingestion pass is added later.
10. Result slides should prefer compact tables or result cards with explicit comparison context, not isolated headline numbers.

## Story spine

1. Start by telling the reader how to use the deck and what question the literature is supposed to answer.
2. Establish the six cost buckets so the rest of the deck has a stable vocabulary.
3. Show a paper-to-bucket map before diving into details, so the reader knows why each paper appears.
4. Slow down the four strongest papers and give each of them a three-slide teaching module:
   - problem and core decomposition or system idea,
   - method/mechanism and evaluation setup,
   - concrete findings, limitations, and repo interpretation, with one result table or result-card cluster that keeps the comparison context visible.
5. Give Cornflakes a two-slide module because its hybrid copy/scatter-gather idea needs actual explanation and a compact evidence slide with mixed-source caveats made explicit.
6. Keep RR-Compound as one explicit low-confidence design-space slide rather than pretending it is a fully taught paper.
7. Bridge from literature to Tonic by mapping the buckets onto the real byte path and instrumentation hooks from repo architecture notes.
8. Revisit repo-local profiling so the literature is connected to observed regimes rather than left abstract.
9. Close with characterization rules and the next measurement pass for Tonic.

The closing message should be:

> the literature gives the decomposition vocabulary and the experimental caution, but the final answer still has to come from a Tonic-specific measurement campaign.

### Slide 5 — **Cloud-Scale RPC Characterization: the decomposition to remember**

- **Purpose:** make the paper's decomposition itself operationally memorable
- **Key takeaway:** the paper first separates non-application latency from application processing, then decomposes the non-application part by request/response direction and by queueing, processing+network-stack work, and wire delay.
- **Components:**
  - left `panel` for why this is the anchor paper:
    - measurement-style paper versus mechanism paper
    - explicit split of completion time into application work and RPC latency tax
  - right staged diagram using stacked `stage-card`s for:
    - end-to-end latency split into application vs tax
    - opening the tax into request/response queues + processing/network-stack + wire
    - explicit mention that average tax and tail tax can diverge

### Slide 6 — **Cloud-Scale RPC Characterization: how the study earns authority**

- **Purpose:** explain why the decomposition is credible for this deck
- **Key takeaway:** a long production window plus three complementary data sources (Monarch, Dapper, GWP) lets the paper compare trends, per-RPC components, and CPU-cycle tax without a single-lens claim.
- **Components:**
  - left methodology flow for:
    - production metrics sampled every `30` minutes for `700` days (`Dec 2020` → `Nov 2022`)
    - multi-source toolchain: Monarch (fleet counters), Dapper (1-day trace depth), and GWP (daily profiles)
    - Dapper method filtering rule (`>= 100` samples for method-level P99)
  - right-side `card`s for:
    - what is being compared (methods, services, clusters, sizes, call trees, latency components, CPU-cycle tax)
    - why long-window plus cross-source triangulation increases trust in the claim hierarchy
    - where limits remain (single-provider stack context)

### Slide 7 — **Cloud-Scale RPC Characterization: findings, limits, and Tonic lesson**

- **Purpose:** make the measurement paper feel concrete rather than conceptual only
- **Key takeaway:** the most actionable lesson is concentration and skew: modest global averages coexist with tail-heavy tax and service/method asymmetries.
- **Components:**
  - one compact 3-column table with:
    - study context
    - reported finding
    - why it matters
  - required rows for:
    - `700`-day growth trend (`~64%` RPC/CPU gain, `~30%` annual growth)
    - heavy latency skew in median and P99 across methods (`90%` services median >= `10.7ms`, etc.)
    - storage-heavy and service-level concentration (`Network Disk` dominance patterns and storage/cycle mix)
    - low-frequency but high-consumption methods (`1000` slowest methods are `1.1%` of calls but `89%` of time)
    - tax decomposition (`2.0%` average tax vs `8.6%` median method ratio; top 10% median `38%`, `P90=96%`)
  - one orange limitation `card`
  - one green Tonic-lesson `card`
