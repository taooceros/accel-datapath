## Goal and audience

Advisory board / project meeting. Audience understands async and accelerators but not necessarily this codebase. Goal: show a complete, evidence-backed research narrative — not just results, but the reasoning chain from thesis to measurement to implication.

## Intended output

A single Typst slide deck (~10–12 slides) that compiles cleanly and tells the full story from thesis through hardware proof-of-thesis through software proof-of-thesis through architecture implications.

## Design goals

- One main idea per slide
- Each slide connects to a source and to the overall thesis
- Numbers are traceable to the listed reports
- No unexplained jargon; define terms the audience may not know
- The "so what" is always explicit at the end of each evidence section

---

## Story spine

1. **Open**: Thesis — the question we started with
2. **Hardware lower bound**: RDMA lesson → batching → DSA proves the thesis at the device level
3. **Stack map**: what the Tonic RPC stack looks like, where the components are
4. **Profiling data**: where does time actually go in end-to-end RPC?
5. **Interpretation**: what does the profile data say about offloadability?
6. **Architecture validation**: does the profile confirm or revise the intended crate split?
7. **Implications and next steps**: what to build and why

## Planned slide sequence

### Slide 1 — Title

Title: "When Async Abstractions Meet Hardware Accelerators: A Profiling Story"
Subtitle: "Thesis, measurement, and implication from DSA to Tonic"
Date: 2026-04-08
Speaker: Hongtao Zhang

### Slide 2 — The thesis

Reuse the thesis callout from the Google interview deck:

> Thesis: accelerator programming wants an async abstraction, but once submission cost is amortized, the async control path itself can become more expensive than the hardware work.

Add a brief framing paragraph:
- Modern accelerators (DSA, IAX, RDMA) are naturally async
- Async frameworks are the right programming model
- But: if batching makes device submission cheap, software overhead becomes visible
- The research question: can today's async APIs carry very high-rate offload efficiently?

Sources: `google_interview_research.typ` lines 30–42

### Slide 3 — RDMA lesson: batching makes submission cheap

Card layout:
- RDMA lesson: one doorbell can dominate at high message rates; batching amortizes that cost
- ~100–200 ns MMIO per doorbell
- Once batching amortizes submission, the hardware path is no longer the bottleneck
- The next question: does the same pattern show up in a full software stack?

This is the intuition pump for why the question matters.

Source: `google_interview_research.typ` slide 3 (the RDMA cards)

### Slide 4 — Hardware lower bound: DSA proves the thesis

Two-panel layout:

LEFT — stdexec optimization experiment:
- Baseline full stdexec: 26.3 Mpps, 38.0 ns/op
- Remove scope.nest() + then(): 41.6 Mpps, 24.0 ns/op (1.6x)
- Remove per-op connect() + start(): 59.9 Mpps, 16.7 ns/op (2.3x)
- Hot-cache ceiling: 84 Mpps / 11.9 ns/op at c=32

RIGHT — Real DSA raw number:
- Intel DSA memmove peak: 48.4 Mops/s at batch=128, c=4, 64 B transfers
- The raw hardware can move data much faster than the software stack can submit it

Bottom callout:
"56% of per-op cost was pure software abstraction overhead — before touching any hardware path."

Sources:
- `006.stdexec_overhead_results.md` — stdexec table
- `010.dsa_hw_eval_smoke_numbers_2026-04-06.md` — 48.4 Mops/s number

### Slide 5 — The research turn

One clear callout:

"The thesis is proven at the hardware interface level. Now: does it show up in a full end-to-end RPC stack?"

Three bullet panel:
- We moved from isolated hardware benchmarks to Tonic gRPC profiling
- Tonic is a realistic software stack: prost + codec + compression + HTTP/2 + tokio
- If the same pattern appears, we need to know which parts of the software stack are the visible cost

This slide bridges from the hardware experiment to the RPC profiling.

### Slide 6 — The Tonic stack (architecture map)

Use stage-card layout showing the full send path:

```
prost encode → Codec/Encoder → optional compression → gRPC frame header → Tower layers → HTTP/2 → tokio socket
```

Overlay each stage with a one-word verdict from the profile:
- prost encode/decode: negligible (0.1%)
- Codec/buffer: dominated by memmove + growth (up to 71%)
- Compression: active CPU work (61%) — offload if HW exists
- HPACK/HTTP/2: small (1.7%)
- Tokio/runtime: H2 futex contention (2.8%)

Label the two interception boundaries:
- Codec/body boundary: cleanest payload hook
- Tower layers: cross-cutting transforms

### Slide 10 — The decision rule

One strong callout (green, centered, large text):

"memmove dominates in medium/large regimes — DMA offload targets the right bottleneck."

Sub-bullets:
- Prost encode/decode is negligible at all sizes — do not touch
- Software buffer management is a prerequisite for effective offload — do it first
- Compression is a compressibility gate problem, not a throughput problem — gate it

### Slide 11 — Summary and open questions

Grid of three cards:
- What we established: batching makes hardware submission cheap; software overhead becomes visible at high rates
- What the profile shows: in Tonic, copy dominates in medium/large; codec is negligible; compression is conditional
- What remains open: internal phase timers (encode/decode/compress/buffer), pooled-buffer variant measurement, streaming mode profiling
