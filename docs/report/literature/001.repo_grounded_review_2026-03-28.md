# Repo-Grounded Literature Review

**Date**: 2026-03-28
**Scope**: DSA/stdexec evidence base, IAX extension path, and accelerator-driven RPC research agenda

## 1. Executive summary

The repository's research plan is not mainly a claim about Intel DSA itself. Its core thesis is that **batching changes the performance regime**: once hardware submission cost is amortized into single-digit nanoseconds per operation, the dominant cost shifts upward into the software framework that prepares, submits, and completes those operations. The strongest local evidence for that thesis is the `dsa-stdexec` benchmark stack, where measured layer-removal experiments reduce the path from roughly 38 ns/op to 16.7 ns/op on mock hardware and transfer to roughly 34 Mpps on real DSA hardware. In the repository's framing, stdexec is the measurement vehicle, not the production target.

The most relevant external literature therefore falls into four groups. First, Intel primary documentation for DSA and IAA is the hardware ground truth for descriptors, work queues, completion semantics, and enablement. Second, a smaller body of direct accelerator and DMA literature helps position why data movement offload, queue depth, and placement policy matter. Third, sender/receiver and low-level async-runtime references explain how a DSA or IAA backend can be surfaced as a composable software abstraction. Fourth, RPC and transport papers matter primarily as the next application layer for the repo's methodology: they are the place where the repository wants to test whether the same regime change and crossover analysis hold end to end.

## 2. What the repository has already established

The local evidence is strongest on one point: for small operations on batched DSA, **software cost dominates hardware cost**. `docs/report/progress_post_alignment_debug.md`, `docs/report/stdexec_overhead_results.md`, and `docs/research_plan.md` all converge on the same picture. The measured delta from `noalloc` to `direct` is about 14 ns/op, attributed to `scope.nest()` plus `then()`, and the delta from `direct` to `reusable` is about 7 ns/op, attributed to `connect()` plus `start()`. The repository explicitly prefers this layer-removal method over speculative phase-by-phase accounting, and `remark/002_layer_removal_measures_abstraction_cost.md` treats that methodological choice as a contribution in its own right.

A second established result is that the software stack matters not only for direct overhead, but also for hardware utilization. The repo's reports argue that reducing framework overhead tightens batches and improves the polling/submission loop enough to raise real hardware throughput, not just mock throughput. Related internal remarks also sharpen the positioning: `remark/007_batching_regime_change_is_general.md` argues the phenomenon should generalize to RDMA, io_uring, NVMe, and GPU submission; `remark/008_composability_not_inherently_expensive.md` reframes the question as a framework design gap rather than an inevitable composability penalty; and `remark/010_stdexec_is_case_study_not_target.md` makes clear that the long-term target is production systems such as gRPC/EventEngine, not stdexec itself.

## 3. Highest-priority external literature for this repo

The first sources to trust are the Intel primary documents. The **Intel DSA Architecture Specification**, **Intel DSA User Guide**, and **Intel DSA Enabling Guide** are the key references for everything in `dsa-stdexec` and `hw-eval`, because they define the device model the repository is measuring and abstracting. On the IAA side, the **Intel IAA Architecture Specification** and **Intel IAA User Guide** are the direct match for the repository's compression and analytics offload ambitions in `accel-rpc` and `hw-eval`. These are not just setup documents; they are the source of truth for completion semantics, batch behavior, work-queue constraints, and operation support.

The next most useful sources are Intel's software stacks and close implementation references. **Intel DML** is the most relevant software-layer reference for DSA-style data movement and async completion patterns, while **Intel QPL** plays the same role for IAA-facing compression pipelines. For the software abstraction side, **NVIDIA stdexec** is the clearest sender/receiver implementation reference, and **libunifex io_uring context examples** show how a low-level async backend can be surfaced as sender-style operations. For the repo's longer-term systems story, the Kubernetes device-plugin docs for **Intel DSA** and **Intel IAA** are also useful because they show how queues are exposed and managed in multi-tenant environments.

The direct peer-reviewed paper set is thinner than the documentation set. The strongest direct match currently identified is **"Intel in-Memory Analytics Accelerator: Performance Characterization and Guidelines"** (2025), which gives a measured view of IAA rather than a product-only view. Around it, two adjacent DMA/offload papers are especially useful for positioning: **IOctopus: Outsmarting Nonuniform DMA** (ASPLOS 2020), which is relevant to topology and placement policy for DMA engines, and **True IOMMU Protection from DMA Attacks: When Copy is Faster than Zero Copy** (2016), which is an older but still useful caution against assuming that offload or zero-copy is automatically beneficial. These adjacent papers matter because the repository's core question is not simply “can DSA move data,” but “under what structural conditions does offload actually improve end-to-end cost?”

## 4. How the literature maps to the repo's three subprojects

For **`dsa-stdexec`**, the literature foundation is already clear. Intel DSA specs and guides provide the hardware semantics. DML, stdexec, and libunifex give the software-integration backdrop. IOctopus and related DMA/offload work provide the closest academic analogs for why scheduling, queue depth, and memory-placement effects matter. This is the most mature part of the repository, and the place where a literature review should spend the most time tying external references back to measured local evidence.

For **`hw-eval`**, the literature is simpler: this subproject is the raw-hardware floor and validation layer. Its purpose is to separate hardware cost from framework cost, so the relevant sources are the DSA and IAA architecture specifications, user guides, and any characterization papers that measure hardware behavior directly. In other words, `hw-eval` should be read as the repository's answer to the question, “what is the hardware floor before framework choices dominate?”

For **`accel-rpc`**, the literature gap is largest and most interesting. The internal plan in `docs/plan/2026-03-05/01_accelerator_driven_rpc.in_progress.md` is concrete about the intended pipeline — CPU serialization/deserialization, IAA compression/decompression, DSA copy+CRC, and async integration with Tonic/Tokio — but the direct academic corpus on accelerator-assisted gRPC or Rust async data-path offload is sparse. The closest direct systems precedent is **RR-Compound: RDMA-Fused gRPC for Low Latency and High Throughput With an Easy Interface** (2024), which is important because it preserves the gRPC surface while accelerating the data path underneath. Around that, the best adjacent papers are **Datacenter RPCs can be General and Fast** (eRPC, NSDI 2019) as the CPU baseline, **Telepathic Datacenters: Fast RPCs using Shared CXL Memory** as a shared-memory direction that explicitly attacks RPC-stack overhead, **OffRAC** as an accelerator-through-RPC systems design, **How to Copy Memory? Coordinated Asynchronous Copy as a First-Class OS Service** as a copy-offload abstraction, and **DSA-2LM** as a recent Intel DSA systems integration result. On the implementation side, the most relevant references are the tonic transport and compression hooks, gRPC's own compression guide, Tokio's sync/async bridging guidance, `spawn_blocking`, and `tokio-uring` as a Rust submission/completion runtime model. This is not a weakness of the plan; it is the clearest statement of the gap the repository is trying to fill.

## 5. Positioning against adjacent systems work

The repo's strongest external positioning is not “another accelerator library,” but a complement to prior host-network work. Internally, `remark/006_research_positioning_notes.md` and `remark/011_mmio_bottleneck_software_vs_hardware_solutions.md` already frame the relationship well: prior work such as **Understanding the Host Network**, **hostCC**, **ZeroNIC**, and **CXL-NIC** characterizes or improves the NIC-to-host path, while this repository targets the **host-to-accelerator offload path**. The common bottlenecks are MMIO cost, cache pressure, queue behavior, and data movement overhead, but the direction through the system is different.

That distinction matters because it also explains why the repo's batching thesis is broader than DSA. If CXL-NIC treats MMIO as a hardware bottleneck and replaces it with coherence messages, this repository treats MMIO as a software-visible bottleneck and amortizes it through batching. The two are complementary. More importantly, once hardware cost is reduced far enough by either route, software framework overhead becomes even more exposed. That is the key reason the repository's claims should be positioned as a framework-regime argument, not merely an accelerator microbenchmarking exercise.

## 6. Gaps and immediate reading priorities

The current literature picture suggests three immediate reading priorities. First, the project should keep Intel primary docs and Intel software stacks at the center of any near-term implementation or benchmarking work, because they are the most reliable grounding for DSA and IAA behavior. Second, for the mature `dsa-stdexec` story, the most useful academic comparisons are not broad networking papers but a small number of DMA/offload and async-infrastructure analogs that speak directly to submission cost, queue behavior, or completion strategy. Third, for `accel-rpc`, the next reading set is now clear: **RR-Compound**, **eRPC**, **Telepathic Datacenters**, **OffRAC**, **Copier**, **DSA-2LM**, plus the tonic/Tokio/gRPC implementation references. That cluster gives the repo both a direct precedent for “keep the RPC API, accelerate below it” and a concrete statement of what is still missing in the literature: a Rust-native, tonic-compatible, accelerator-aware async data path.

Based on the current evidence, the repo already has a defensible literature-backed claim for `dsa-stdexec`: batching exposes software overhead that was previously hidden, and layer-removal is a sound way to measure it. The open literature task is to complete the equivalent grounding for the RPC path, where the likely contribution is less “directly reproducing prior work” and more “transferring a validated methodology and a batching-regime lens into an underexplored accelerator-assisted RPC setting.”

## 8. Current conclusion

The literature now supports two distinct claims. The first is already strong and directly grounded: for batched DSA, the repository has both local measurements and enough external support to argue that submission amortization exposes framework overhead as the dominant cost. The second is a forward-looking but credible research gap: there is still very little direct work on **Rust tonic/gRPC with integrated accelerator offload for copy, checksum, and compression under an async completion model**. That gap is precisely why `accel-rpc` is interesting. The literature around it is adjacent enough to justify the plan, but not so complete that the repo would merely be reimplementing an established design.
